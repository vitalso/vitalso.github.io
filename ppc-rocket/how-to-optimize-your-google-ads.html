How to Optimize Your Google Ads with Keyword Grouping

Introduction

Google Ads is a powerful tool that, when used correctly, can help your business succeed and grow. However, there are many different aspects of the platform that require careful consideration to get right. In this post, we will explain how to optimize your Google Ads through keyword grouping.

What is keyword grouping?

Keyword grouping is the process of organizing your target keywords in Google Ads into smaller and more manageable groups known as Ad Groups. Each Ad Group will contain one or more ads that share similar targets and each Campaign is made up of one or more Ad Groups. For each Campaign, you can have 20,000 Ad Groups and each can include up to 50 ads.

For each Ad Group, you can set a cost-per-click (CPC), which is the amount you are willing to spend to trigger ads to appear when a relevant term is searched in Google. You can also set the CPC for each individual keyword in the Ad Group.

Ad Groups can be grouped in a number of ways, as we will explore later in this post. However, the most common method is to create Ad Groups for keywords of a similar topic.

Why is keyword grouping so important for Google Ads?

The more specific you make with your keyword groupings the more tailored your ads can be. This is because, by grouping your keywords into smaller, more manageable groups, it becomes easier to target your ads and ensure they are relevant to the people who are searching for them.

This is important because it helps you to:

  1. Avoid wasting time and money on ads that no one will see or promoting ads that are not relevant enough.
  2. Improve your click-through rate (CTR), which is the percentage of people who click on your ad after seeing it. A higher CTR means that more people are finding your ad relevant and useful, which can lead to more sales or leads.
  3. Improve relevance, both in your ads and in the landing pages they link to. This will mean an improved quality score which leads to a better price per click and often leads to more conversions too.
  4. Better track your results and see which keywords are performing well so you can adjust your campaigns accordingly.

How to group keywords?

By topic

As previously mentioned, the most common way to group keywords is by topic. For example, if you're a clothing retailer, you might group your keywords into separate groups for men's clothing, women's clothing, and children's clothing. This makes it easier to target your ads and ensure that they're relevant to the people who are searching on Google.

Depending on how many types of products you are offering, and how many you want to pay to advertise, you may choose to be more specific with your keyword groupings. For example, creating a group for "men’s shorts" or even a separate group for the different uses of the shorts e.g. "men’s sports shorts" and "men’s formal shorts".

By match type

Another method is to group keywords by match type. Match types are the different ways your ad can appear in search results. There are three main match types: broad match, phrase match, and exact match.

Broad match

Broad match is the default match type. It allows your ads to appear for any variation of your keyword, even if it's not an exact match. For example, for a board match keywords like “men’s shorts”, you are likely to show up for searches like “swim shorts”, “chino shorts”, “men’s linen shorts”, etc.

With this match type, you can attract more visitors to your website and spend less time building keyword lists. However, you may find yourself attracting less relevant searchers which can affect your conversions. Fortunately, once you have been running your campaigns for a little while you should have some data available to help you figure out which of the broad match keywords are most productive and should be concentrated on with higher spending and which should be added to your negative keyword lists.

Exact match

Exact match requires the keyword to be the only word in the search query. So, for the example of "men’s shorts" only searches like “mens shorts”, “shorts for men”, “shorts men” and ‘men shorts’ will display the ads. This match type gives you the most control but is also likely to reach fewer searchers than either of the other match types.

Phrase match

Phrase match requires that the keyword be included in the search query, but also allows other words to be included. For example, when set to phrase match, the same ‘men’s shorts’ keyword would likely display ads for “shorts for men”, “men’s chino shorts”, “mens blue shorts”, etc.

This type of match is the mid-point between broad match and exact match, so your ads will likely be displayed for more searchers for exact match and less than broad match. This also means your ads are more likely to appear to more relevant searchers than the broad match but less than with the exact match.

Keep in mind that you can and should use a combination of these methods to group your keywords. For example, you could create a group for all of your broad match keywords about men's clothing and another group for all of your phrase match keywords about women's clothing. Or you could create a group for all of your exact match keywords about children's clothing. But keep in mind that adding negative keywords to the respective Ad Groups will help you avoid spending your marketing budget inefficiently.

How to optimize your Google Ads with keyword grouping

If you're not already using keyword grouping in your Google Ads campaigns, now is the time to start. Here's how to do it:

  1. Identify Your Keywords: The first step is to identify the keywords that you want to target in your ads. Make a list of all the relevant terms that potential customers might use when searching for your products or services.
  2. Group Similar Keywords Together: Once you have a list of keywords, group them based on similarity. For example, you might group all the keywords related to a particular product or service or all the keywords that are variations of each other (e.g. "men’s trousers" and "trousers for men").
  3. Create Ads for Each Group: Now that you've grouped your keywords, you can create ads specifically targeted to each group. This ensures that your ads are relevant to the searcher's query and more likely to be clicked on.
  4. Monitor Your Results: Finally, don't forget to monitor your results so you can see which keyword groups are performing well and adjust your campaigns accordingly. By using keyword grouping, you can more easily track and optimize your Google Ads campaigns to continually improve results.

5 Keyword grouping tips to get you started

Not sure where to begin when optimizing your keyword groups? Here are a few tips to help you get started and quickly improve your results.

Use PPC Rocket’s keyword grouping tool

At PPC Rocket we offer a fantastic tool to help you make the right decisions regarding your keyword grouping.

Consider your website

Many businesses follow the layout of their website when arranging Campaigns and Ad Groups. For example, you could take the product categories in your navigation bar and the sub-categories that your users can drill down to. Similarly grouping your keywords can help you ensure you are not missing out on anything important and select the most relevant landing pages to link your ads to.

Keep your groups relevant

Make sure that each group contains keywords that are highly relevant to each other. This will help ensure that your ads are targeted and relevant to the people who see them.

Don't overfill your Ad Groups

Don't try to cram too many different keywords into each group. This will make it harder to target your ads effectively and could result in lower CTRs. Most specialists agree that 3-20 keywords per group is usually a good rule of thumb. However, this depends on the keyword variations and how similar they are. Remember, you can have up to 20,000 Ad Groups per Campaign so you can be as specific as you like.

Use negative keywords

In addition to grouping together similar keywords, you can also use negative keywords to exclude certain searches from triggering your ads. These can be added at the Campaign or Ad Group level. We recommend you think carefully about which negative keywords you do not want ads in a specific Ad Group so show up for. For example, if you have broad match Ad Groups for women's bags and one exact match ad group for women's bags on sale in the same Campaign you may want to add "sale" to the women's bags Ad Group's negative keywords list. However, you would not want to add that at the campaign level, which would block all ads in the Campaign from appearing for those searches.
