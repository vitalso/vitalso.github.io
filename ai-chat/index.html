Question 1: Why is the sky blue? The sky appears blue to the human eye during the daytime due to a phenomenon called Rayleigh scattering. This occurs when the Earth's atmosphere scatters sunlight in all directions. Blue light is scattered more than other colors because it travels as shorter, smaller waves. This is why we see a blue sky most of the time. Question 2: Why is the sky red? The sky can appear red, especially during sunrise and sunset, due to the same scattering of lightâ€”only in these instances, the sun's light has to pass through more of the Earth's atmosphere due to the angle of the sun relative to the observer. Longer wavelengths of light (reds and oranges) are less scattered by the atmosphere than the shorter blue wavelengths. When the sun is low on the horizon, sunlight passes through more air at a lower angle and more blue light is scattered out of the direct line of sight, leaving the longer red wavelengths to dominate the observed spectrum. Additionally, if there are more particles or pollution in the atmosphere, they can enhance the red color of the sky at these times because they scatter more light.
